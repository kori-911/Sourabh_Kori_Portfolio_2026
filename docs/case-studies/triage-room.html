A supply chain crisis exposed the gap between Toyota's operational complexity and its collaboration tooling

Toyota Motor North America manages 400+ suppliers and thousands of part-supplier-plant intersections. When a critical part faces a supply gap — a semiconductor shortage, a natural disaster hitting a supplier location, a logistics breakdown — the resolution process was unstructured: scattered emails, phone calls, and Excel files sitting on individual laptops.

Competitors (Resilinc's "War Room", Blue Yonder's "Situation Room") had similar offerings. After a 2-year vendor evaluation against SAP, Kinaxis, and Blue Yonder, Toyota selected o9 Solutions for its 2-Way Supplier Collaboration (2WSC) initiative. Triage Room was identified as a key differentiator.

Three user archetypes, two organizations, one closed room

I mapped the problem space across three user archetypes within the multi-tier supply network, each with distinct jobs-to-be-done and information asymmetries:

PSD Lead Engineer (OEM)

  • Identify supply gaps from forecast data
  • Escalate critical shortages to the right supplier contact
  • Assess production impact at NAMC plants
  • Track resolution progress across multiple commodities
  • Close rooms and manage watchlist designations

Supplier Lead Planner

  • Receive notification of gap escalation
  • Report run-out details and sub-component bottlenecks
  • Provide transportation and labor constraint information
  • Collaborate on countermeasures in real-time
  • Update supplier commits based on resolution

PSD Risk Management

  • Monitor all open rooms across the portfolio
  • Assess risk rank trends and aging
  • Generate executive summaries by commodity
  • Understand resolution patterns for continuous improvement
  • Manage watchlist at the 5-digit part level
The core design tension: Two organizations (buyer and supplier) with asymmetric information, different permission models, and competing urgencies — needing to collaborate in a single, privacy-controlled space where even platform admins cannot see the conversation.

Designing for a system, not a screen

Triage Room doesn't exist in isolation. It sits at the intersection of four platform subsystems, and my design decisions had to account for how data and user actions flow across all of them. I mapped the system architecture early to identify coupling points and constraint boundaries.

Subsystem dependencies I designed around

Collaboration (Pulse)

  • Each room creates a unique Pulse folder. Folder naming was a design decision — we chose display name over LS key for readability, with the member ID stored as a tag for programmatic lookup. Folder access is user-level, not role-level, ensuring privacy even from admins.

Data Security (ACL)

  • I designed the permission model: suppliers can only write to Run-Out measures when room state is "Open"; buyers can only write to NAMC Impact measures when state is "Open". Both lose write access when the room closes. This required 40+ ACL rules I helped specify.

Live Server (LS)

  • Triage Room is modeled as its own dimension with association measures linking to Item, Supplier Location, and Plant. State changes trigger parameterized IBPL rules. I designed the state machine to ensure the UI could reliably reflect lifecycle stages.

SailPoint Integration

  • OEM Lead auto-assignment pulls from SailPoint data (POD-to-user mapping). I designed the form to show the suggested lead while allowing override — balancing automation with human judgment in a crisis context where the default person may not be the right one.

Trade-offs that shaped the product

Every design decision in an enterprise context is a trade-off between user ideal, engineering feasibility, timeline, and platform consistency. Here are the five decisions that had the most impact:

Reuse existing widgets vs. build custom components

KC's direction was clear: leverage existing Pulse, inline form, and pivot infrastructure. This wasn't just a speed play — it meant suppliers already familiar with the o9 platform wouldn't learn new patterns. I redesigned around composable widget layouts, reducing engineering effort by ~40% while maintaining full requirements coverage. The trade-off: less visual differentiation, but faster time-to-value and lower risk.

  • Design System Reuse
  • Risk Reduction

Contextual creation vs. standalone workflow

Positioned "Create Triage Room" directly on the Forecast Commit Gap pivot. Row context (Part, Supplier Location, OEM Location) auto-populates the form. This reduces errors and makes room creation feel like a natural extension of gap analysis. The alternative — a standalone creation page — would have been more flexible but disconnected from the user's diagnostic context.

  • Contextual Design
  • Error Reduction

User-level access control vs. role-based

Each Triage Room generates a unique collaboration folder with per-user ACL rules (not role-based). Even platform admins can't access others' chats. This was a Toyota hard requirement driven by supplier confidentiality concerns — a supplier's gap details should never be visible to a competing supplier or to users outside the room. The design implication: explicit participant management directly in the creation form, with dynamic ACL rules generated via JavaScript at room creation time.

  • Privacy by Design
  • ACL Architecture

Dual-audience forms with strict data ownership

Run-Out & Supply Gap is supplier-editable only; NAMC Impact Evaluation is OEM-editable only. Both sides can see each other's data but can't modify it. This maps to real accountability — suppliers own their gap analysis, Toyota owns production impact assessment. Closed rooms make all forms read-only. I specified the ACL rules to scope write access to room state == "Open" only.

  • Accountability Mapping
  • State-Dependent Permissions

State machine design for room lifecycle

Designed a rule-engine-driven state machine where transitions depend on: current state, user type, and whether mandatory measures have been filled. For example, OEM can change state to "OEM Essential Info Given" only if all required NAMC Impact fields are non-null. Supplier cannot close a room — only OEM can. This prevents premature resolution and ensures both parties have contributed before closure.

  • Workflow Design
  • Governance

The biggest architectural shift: moving from a task-management-centric design to a collaboration-first approach. Supply crisis resolution is fundamentally a conversation, not a task list. The restructured design mirrors how these problems actually get solved: through rapid back-and-forth communication supported by structured data capture.

Building on the o9 component library, not around it

A key constraint — and ultimately a strength — was building within o9's existing design system. Rather than introducing new components, I composed the Triage Room experience from existing primitives:

Existing Components Used

  • Pulse Widget (chat, tasks, files)
  • Inline Form (read-only summary)
  • Pivot Table (data grids)
  • Action Buttons (create, close, update)
  • KPI Cards (dashboard metrics)
  • Global/Row Filters

New Patterns Introduced

  • Triage Room indicator icon on pivot rows
  • Composite widget layout (Pulse + Form + Grid)
  • Closed-state behavior (disabled banner + readonly)
  • Form completion status indicators (✓ / ⚠)
  • Chip-based intersection selection in forms
  • Parent-child pivot for drill-down data

Platform Enhancements Requested

  • "Show triage rooms" toggle in pivot config
  • Triage Room dimension navigation link
  • Composite config for side-by-side widgets
  • Folder tagging for programmatic lookup
  • Email notification from measure changes
  • EnableAlphaFeatures tenant setting

The "Platform Enhancements Requested" column is important — it shows where the design exposed gaps in the existing component library that needed R&D investment. These weren't arbitrary asks; each was tied to a Toyota requirement that couldn't be met with existing primitives. I worked with R&D to scope and prioritize these as separate engineering stories within the PI.

Five key screens that power the Triage Room

The final design spans a dedicated workspace with a dashboard for monitoring all rooms, a detail view for active collaboration, and specialized tabs for structured data capture from both buyer and supplier sides.

Triage Room Dashboard

Master list with OEM Lead, Supplier Lead, Rank, State, Supplier, and Parts. Expandable rows for multi-part intersections. Tabs for Dashboard (operational) and Analytics (historical).

Create Triage Room Form

Modal triggered from pivot table row context. Auto-populated fields: Opened On, OEM Lead (via SailPoint), Parts Location (chip-based removable intersections). Pre-validation: single supplier per room.

Triage Room Detail View

Split layout: Pulse chat (posts, files, tags, tasks, comments) on the left. Summary panel on the right: room metadata, completion status indicators, Affected Parts grid.

Run-Out & Supply Gap (Supplier Tab)

Supplier-editable pivot: First Missed Shipdate, Sub Component, Transportation, Labor constraints. Scoped to open rooms only via ACL.

NAMC Impact Evaluation (OEM Tab)

Toyota-editable form: Vehicle Retrofit, Lead Time to NAMC, On-Hand Condition, Production Condition, Days Behind Schedule. Visible to suppliers as read-only.

What I learned, what I'd change

Working as the sole designer on a feature touching Toyota's production operations was the most intense project of my career. I was simultaneously the person interviewing stakeholders, mapping data models, designing screens, presenting to clients, and specifying ACL rule implications for the engineering team.

What I'd do differently

Direct supplier user research. Most of our supplier understanding came through Toyota's lens. Direct feedback from supplier-side users on the Run-Out form — which has 15+ fields — would have helped prioritize progressive disclosure and identify which fields are actually filled first in a crisis.

Earlier design system contribution. The new patterns I introduced (composite widget layouts, closed-state behaviors, completion indicators) were validated through the Triage Room project but could have been contributed back to the o9 design system earlier, enabling reuse in other SRM features.

What I'm proud of

The design survived contact with reality. The core interaction model I established in those first two weeks — contextual creation from the gap table, split-view collaboration, dual-audience forms, state-machine-driven lifecycle — remained intact through to the shipped product and through horizontal productization. That architectural resilience came from investing in systems thinking upfront rather than jumping to screens.

The feature shipped on time within the PI, was adopted by 4,000+ users, was productized horizontally, and became a selling point in deals with Apple, Marelli, and beyond.
